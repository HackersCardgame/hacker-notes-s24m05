TCP and UDP are two different ways that packets of data on a network (such as the Internet) are packaged, sent, and received.

TCP is the more robust of the two; it detects whether data packets might have been dropped or mixed in order on their way to a destination, and if so, the destination will nag the source to resend lost packets.

For some applications, though (such as audio/video streaming), you might not necessarily /want/ to ensure that every last packet gets resent until it arrives. UDP, unlike TCP, simply breaks data up into packets and sends it with little to no further processing; the application is left with the task of ordering packets and/or requesting dropped packets, if it cares to do so.

As such, when a packet is sent through UDP, there is no particular assurance that it will get to its destination if it were to get mishandled somewhere. In other words, if someone sends you a joke via UDP, you might not get it.
