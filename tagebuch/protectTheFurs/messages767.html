Scalar instructions generally access scalar registers (SGPRs) - there are around 100 of these, but they are shared among all 64 lanes of the CU. Every CU has an SALU (for scalar arithmetic logic unit) and a VALU (vector ALU), which are four-staged pipelines.
Now... there are three major resources which a compute kernel has on a CU. Vector registers, scalar registers, and on-chip SRAM called LDS (Local Data Share, or sometimes, "shared memory".) If, for any reason, your compute kernel stalls (maybe a memory access, maybe a long-running instruction), the scheduler may run another kernel on that same CU, provided that there is enough of those three resources. Now... this can also be done multiple times! If BOTH kernels stall, then a third may be run on that CU, while the other two must wait. For this to work at all, though, there is a catch: You can't clobber any of the resources that another kernel is using.
This means, to have two "waves in flight" (kernels on the same CU), your kernel (assuming the kernels are the same) must use less than half of the VGPRs, less than half of the SGPRs, and less than half of the available LDS memory. This makes sure the CU can manage by giving each kernel the resources it requires without running out.
