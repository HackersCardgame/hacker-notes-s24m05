Apologies for somewhat selling my philosophy here, but the Taiumvirate approaches goals by meditating on the truth, self, and target.

Truth: People are motivated by emotion - anyone who says they're a purely logical being, I'm curious to know your answer to "Why do you value logic instead of emotional impulsive decision making, when you share 99% of the brain of someone who does make decisions with emotional impulse?". People have negative emotional reactions to people who seem "off" - including talking too slowly. Being able to guide the emotional state of people helps you get what you want from them - be it making people like you so they give you stuff, or raise a friend out of sadness because you want their life to be less miserable.

Self: The ingroup and tools I have. I have sat in front of a psychologist and undergone a 3 hour battery of tests and they have a 16 page document saying my symptoms overlap with autism symptoms. I have trouble translating my thoughts into words fast enough for an average human conversation.

Target: I want to be able to better communicate with people, and I want an exact, equitable, and evidence-based way to improve that - so that I can guide the emotional state of people in front of me. I cannot easily improve the speed of my thought-to-speech rate after years of trying. It's not equitable to me to make myself suffer more given how much time I've spent on that. But. I can practice by talking with people more, and doing things like forming pre-baked phrases and conversation paths so I don't have to use my brain's horsepower scrambling to form words. I can challenge myself to get evidence that this works by going to a games night with furries. If I go full nerd I can quantify statistics like minutes per stranger before they get bored and stop talking - but that doesn't serve my target in a useful way. But I can practice improving my speech rate by relying less on overclocking my brain CPU trying to come up with phrases - and having my brain use pre-determined phrases, as well as practicing it. I can ask my friends if they notice a difference before and after to see if it's actually a problem or not (keeping in mind the level of evidence - do i have friends who will tell me truths I do not want to hear? are they worse at social situations than me?)


To answer your question - I'd consider your own truths, self, and targets, crafting target goals that are exact, equitable, and evidence-based. Autism is not an exact thing to target - your symptoms are. You do not know if your communication issues are due to autism, or culture, or a sky fairy cursing you. The specific things that you want in life that will make you happy are your final targets: being financially secure to afford a certain standard of living. From there, craft more targets including ones that are in your face - right now, if you think communication is an issue, you can immediately walk out the door and into your local game shop and talk with the shopkeep and see what happens. You can PM your friends immediately and ask about their opinions on if your communication is actually a problem.

Nice use of learned responses to help people read what you wrote. I like the breaking up of details into paragraphs as it lowers the chance of the "wall-of-text".

There's an acronym that was used recently which reminded me of it (I have huge problems with acronyms as I have a bunch of issues with language and spelling in general, but that's separate)... SMART - Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic, Timely

Specific - the goal or issue you want to achieve or work on

Measurable - do you have a criteria you can use to measure progress. Improving social interaction is really difficult as someone who's seeing the same person over a long period of time will not be able to notice any changes until they take time to think about it in context. That said, if you have some friends you only talk to sometimes, like every few months, those are the people who you can ask after talking with them for a bit - they have "contrast" though it's still difficult.

Achievable - Are you asking for something you're not capable of? For me, I've had a goal of learning how to spell the words I use every day for all my life - I know that I'll never know them completely and so I wouldn't say my goal of getting spelling right always is achievable (or realistic, or timely) so be a bit kind on yourself as well - If you're very slow to improve on some areas, see if you can find ways to externalize them - I can't write without a computer with spellchecking.

Realistic - Changing something fundamental like how you socialize and work with others is difficult and will take time and practice. It's achievable but be aware of the challenge. Again, be kind to yourself.

Timely - Set a time where you'd like to be complete. This is more of a required thing for people who can't sense time (anyone with ADHD) but it's still valuable for anyone.

The best is if you can set a goal that's a small step from where you are now depending on what the impediment is. Give yourself a reasonable window of time to work on it knowing that it will not be as fast as you will want but at the same time the goal isn't so far out that you'll feel it'll never come or will forget about the goal.
